Training an NH AI opponent for browser PvP practice

NH Trainer is a browser practice client for fighting a trained sparring partner. This blog documents the practical work behind NH AI training, OSRS-style PvP practice, policy checkpoints, and the process of making the opponent more useful over time.

Why the bot uses a learned policy

NH fights are too reactive for a fixed rotation to stay interesting. The opponent needs to adapt to timing, pressure, and the player's rhythm rather than simply looping through a script.

The training process uses many paired fights and records a rolling window of recent combat state. That short memory lets rewards connect an action to future outcomes: a good freeze, a defensive gear swap, a delayed special attack, or a supply decision may only prove useful several ticks later.

What the AI watches during training

The policy observes a compact version of the fight, including current prayers, health, supplies, gear style, weapon timing, distance, freeze state, special attack state, and recent damage pressure. It can choose attacks, gear, prayers, movement, supplies, and special attack intentions through the same kind of game loop the browser client uses.

  • Gear decisions include weapon and defensive setup context rather than a single hard-coded outfit.
  • Movement decisions include standing under, stepping out, and maintaining useful attack distance.
  • Special attack decisions are trained separately enough that AGS timing is not treated like ordinary weapon pressure.

Where training got confusing

A lot of the hard parts were not glamorous model work. The confusing bugs were usually small parity issues: a bot getting information one tick too early, a weapon swap not executing through the same action path, or a melee path that looked almost right but changed the timing of a real fight. Those bugs can make a policy look worse than it is, because the model is trying to act through a world that does not quite match the one it trained in.

The fix was to keep tightening the environment around the model. When the action space, observations, tick timing, and movement rules line up, the learned behavior becomes much easier to judge.

Difficulty checkpoints

The public client currently exposes multiple policy checkpoints. Easy, medium, and hard are not hand-authored personalities; they are snapshots taken after different amounts of training and then served as selectable opponents.
